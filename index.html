Thanks for visiting! I sociologist using computational and qualitative methods to study sites where the passions and politics of crime meet the cold gears of bureaucracy. This has led me to investigate the increasingly central role of nonprofits in the American state, the politics and administration of violence, and the idiosyncracies of local government. I am a post-doctoral fellow working with the Complexity and Society Lab at the Northeastern University Network Science Institute and the Institute for Policing, Incarceration, and Public Safety at Harvard University. I am also a fellow at the Harvard Department of Sociology. I received my PhD in sociology from the University of California, Berkeley in 2025.

My dissertation and current book project investigates efforts to cultivate community-based violence prevention projects in California since 1990. Through fifteen months of ethnographic fieldwork, 72 interviews, and extensive archival research, I show how a rigid system of competitive nonprofit contracting emerged and consolidated to make it increasingly easy for cities to experiment with new, non-policing approaches to public safety, but difficult for their proponents to institutionalize them. In doing so, I draw out the interrelations of state and street violence, the role of nonprofits in the modern American state, and the predicaments of criminal justice reform for both advocates on the ground and theorists of the penal state.

I also write on bureaucratic decision-making, moral meaning-making around violence, and policy reform using parole boards as the primary case. This work looks the reasons for granting or denying parole to people committed to to prison for violent offenses, and how those reasons change over time. This includes a paper on how old rehabilitative ideas become new again and one (with Brendon McConnell, currently an R&R at the American Journal of Sociology) on how parole commissioners respond to signals that they should be harsher in their decisions. Another set of papers (with Joss Greene) excavate the moral categories and logics of parole decision-making. This work highlights the categorical fluidity of individuals up for parole, the racialized gender tropes that commissioners mobilize to justify their decisions, and the symbolic value of incarcerated labor.

Building on my interest in the intersection of local government and public safety, in my postdoctoral fellowship I am leading efforts to develop the Atlas of Municipal Courts. The Atlas is a project to collect and make publicly available data on all municipal courts in the U.S. for use by academics, advocates, and members of the public. Municipal courts are run directly by city governments, rather than state court systems, and handle a wide range of subfelony enforcement. In recent years, they have attracted increasing scrutiny due to their potential for abuse as a revenue source for cities through fines and fees—as happened in Ferguson, Missouri in the early 2010s. This project promises to shed light on the entanglements of local law enforcement, municipal governance, and inequality.

Elsewhere, I am currently at work on a paper that probes justifications for police violence (with Sandra Smith and Jaqueline Lepe), and one that identifies the problems generated by the causal revolution in policy evaluation research to studying interpersonal violence and violence prevention programs.

Across all my work, I am interested in the dynamics of institutional change, the possibilities for more safe and fair approaches to public safety, and the implications for reducing social inequality. If you'd like to get in touch, please send me an email: idalke -at- fas.harvard.edu.
